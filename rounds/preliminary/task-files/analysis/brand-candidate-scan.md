# Highlighted Brand Candidate Scan

## Purpose

This document is a first-pass scan of the highlighted L'Oreal brands for preliminary-round brand selection.

The goal is not to decide immediately, but to clarify:

- each brand's core DNA
- its possible strategic positioning in China Gen Z interaction
- where the brand-consumer relationship can be reconstructed
- whether AI can become a strategic relationship infrastructure rather than a feature wrapper

## Selection Lens

For this case, the best brand should satisfy five conditions:

1. Clear relationship gap: the brand has a visible tension with Gen Z consumers.
2. Strong brand DNA: the solution can only belong to this brand, not any L'Oreal brand.
3. AI relevance: AI can reshape relationship quality, not just improve targeting or recommendation.
4. Commercial path: the idea can connect to conversion, retention, content, membership, or channel growth.
5. Deck distinctiveness: the brand can generate a memorable story within 8 PPT pages.

## Brand Notes

### 1. L'Oreal Paris

**Brand DNA**

L'Oreal Paris is positioned by L'Oreal as the world's No. 1 beauty brand, built around transformative beauty, pioneering science, French savoir-faire, and the long-running "I'm Worth It" empowerment mission. Its official brand page emphasizes makeup, skincare, haircare, hair color, and self-worth across women of all ages, skin tones, and hair types.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

For Gen Z, L'Oreal Paris could be positioned as:

> the everyday beauty confidence partner that helps each person translate self-worth into daily beauty choices.

**Relationship opportunity**

The brand's strength is scale, trust, and broad category coverage. The relationship gap is that a very broad empowerment message may feel too mass and less intimate to Gen Z. AI could help make "I'm Worth It" more personal: not a slogan, but a daily confidence system across skin, makeup, hair, and occasion.

**Risk**

It may be too broad for an 8-page case. The answer could easily become a group-level L'Oreal strategy rather than a sharp brand-specific strategy.

### 2. Maybelline New York

**Brand DNA**

Maybelline is positioned as the No. 1 makeup brand in the world, rooted in New York energy, no-hassle high-performance makeup, democratic beauty, diversity, and self-expression. The official page also highlights its Brave Together mental health initiative and its focus on possibility.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the accessible urban self-expression brand that helps Gen Z move between moods, scenes, and identities without overthinking makeup.

**Relationship opportunity**

Maybelline is naturally close to social makeup behavior: quick looks, trend replication, affordable experimentation, and creator-led content. AI could support "ready for anything" makeup navigation: commute, date, interview, concert, gym, livestream, bad-skin day, low-energy day.

**Risk**

It is a strong mass makeup brand, but the strategic idea may overlap with many trend-based makeup brands. Need a sharper Chinese-market relationship gap, otherwise it risks becoming "AI look recommendation."

### 3. 3CE Stylenanda

**Brand DNA**

3CE was created in 2009 inside Stylenanda, a Seoul street-fashion label. L'Oreal describes it as the No. 1 Korean makeup brand in the world, "hot from Seoul," born indie and now global. Its official DNA includes fashion-forward, made of color, and playfully creative.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the AI style co-creation partner that helps Chinese Gen Z turn mood, scene, outfit, and social identity into a shareable beauty look.

**Relationship opportunity**

This is the strongest candidate for "relationship reconstruction." 3CE's relationship is not primarily about efficacy or prestige; it is about style, identity, color, and social self-presentation. AI can move beyond virtual try-on into co-creating a style persona, content script, product combination, and UGC challenge.

**Why it fits the brief**

The brief asks for AI to reshape relationships. 3CE lets us argue that Gen Z beauty decisions are no longer just "what product suits me," but "what version of myself do I want to perform today?" That is a relationship problem, not a product problem.

**Risk**

The solution can become too campaign-like. We must connect it to measurable growth: UGC quality, style profile creation, product bundle conversion, membership retention, and trend feedback to product/content teams.

### 4. Lancome

**Brand DNA**

Lancome is built on French elegance, happiness, science, confidence, and premium beauty. L'Oreal's official page emphasizes heritage meeting the future of beauty, scientific excellence, inclusive innovation, sustainable luxury, and beauty as a source of happiness and self-expression.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the luxury beauty ritual brand that helps young consumers turn skincare and makeup into confidence, happiness, and self-care rituals.

**Relationship opportunity**

AI could personalize luxury skincare rituals, emotional beauty routines, and high-touch membership services. The relationship reconstruction angle would be from "luxury product ownership" to "luxury care relationship."

**Risk**

Lancome is powerful but mature. It may be harder to show a sharp Gen Z relationship rupture unless we focus on young luxury entrants, first anti-aging, or emotional skincare rituals.

### 5. YSL Beauty

**Brand DNA**

YSL Beauty's official positioning is daring beauty, boldness, singularity, authenticity, liberation from convention, and self-expression. L'Oreal describes the brand as young, edgy, and luxury. Recent coverage also notes YSL Beauty's Gen Z playbook around participatory, local, cultural experiences rather than purely influencer-led luxury events.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the luxury self-expression brand that gives Gen Z culturally charged moments to live, show, and share their own definition of beauty.

**Relationship opportunity**

YSL is a strong candidate because its DNA already points to self-expression and rule-breaking. AI could help consumers translate identity, mood, cultural interests, and city moments into YSL-coded looks, content, and offline participation.

**Risk**

The AI idea must feel premium and culturally intelligent. If it becomes a generic AI generator, it will cheapen the luxury brand. The execution should be event/culture/membership-led rather than tool-led.

### 6. Armani Beauty

**Brand DNA**

Armani Beauty is rooted in authenticity, restraint, simplicity, timeless elegance, cinematic sensuality, and high quality. Its official page highlights minimalist packaging, intuitive formulas, the "no makeup" makeup look, and ties to cinema.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the refined confidence brand for Gen Z consumers who want quiet luxury, natural sophistication, and scene-appropriate elegance.

**Relationship opportunity**

AI could work as a refined look curator: translating life scenes into understated makeup, fragrance, and skincare rituals. This fits young professionals and quiet-luxury consumers.

**Risk**

The relationship reconstruction may be subtler and less distinctive for a competition deck. Armani is elegant, but the AI strategy may feel less urgent than 3CE or YSL unless we anchor it in young professionals' identity transition.

### 7. Kiehl's

**Brand DNA**

Kiehl's is positioned as "Finest Apothecary Skincare," with personalized and specifically formulated skincare, expert-tested routines, community care, and 175 years of knowledge. The official page emphasizes skincare expertise, local communities, inclusivity, sustainability, and expert education through content.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the trusted skin knowledge friend that helps Gen Z build a practical, personalized, low-anxiety skincare routine.

**Relationship opportunity**

This brand is strong for "long-term relationship." AI could convert Kiehl's apothecary expertise into a continuous skincare learning and routine system: personalized routine building, ingredient education, skin diary, community Q&A, and refill/retention prompts.

**Risk**

It may overlap with dermatological beauty brands like La Roche-Posay or CeraVe. To make Kiehl's distinctive, we should emphasize apothecary warmth, community, and lifestyle skincare rather than medical authority.

### 8. Shu Uemura

**Brand DNA**

Shu Uemura is an artist-led Japanese makeup brand built around self-exploration, makeup artistry, tools, colors, craftsmanship, and Asian beauty. L'Oreal's page explicitly says the brand empowers Asian millennials for self-exploration and encourages them to claim their point of view and differences.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the Asian artistry brand that helps Gen Z treat the face as a canvas for personal beauty exploration.

**Relationship opportunity**

This is a creatively rich candidate. AI could act as an artistry mentor: teach techniques, map face-as-canvas looks, suggest color combinations, and help users explore personal beauty rather than follow social rules.

**Risk**

It may be more niche than 3CE, with a smaller mass relevance. The strategy would need to lean into artistry, education, and premium tools, not broad social virality.

### 9. L'Oreal Professionnel

**Brand DNA**

L'Oreal Professionnel is rooted in professional hair expertise, salon relationships, innovation, and beauty tech. Its official page describes it as serving hairstylists and their clients since 1909, partnering with 2.5 million hairstylists and more than 130,000 salons globally, and leading professional hair with cutting-edge technology.

**Possible Gen Z positioning**

> the salon-to-digital hair transformation partner that connects Gen Z consumers, stylists, and hair health through AI.

**Relationship opportunity**

This is an underrated candidate because it avoids the obvious makeup/skincare route. AI could reshape the relationship among consumer, stylist, salon, and brand: hair diagnosis, inspiration translation, color risk simulation, post-salon maintenance, stylist education, and repeat visit management.

**Risk**

The case asks for interaction with China Gen Z consumers. L'Oreal Professionnel's direct relationship is mediated by hairstylists and salons, so the consumer relationship is B2B2C. This can be a strength if handled well, but it makes execution more complex.

## Initial Recommendation

### Best creative-strategic fit: 3CE

3CE is the strongest first-choice candidate if we want a memorable, Gen Z-native, relationship-first strategy. It gives us the cleanest story:

> From selling color products to helping Gen Z co-create style identities.

AI can be positioned as a style relationship infrastructure, not a product feature.

### Best premium cultural fit: YSL Beauty

YSL is strong if the team wants a luxury route with sharper cultural attitude. The story would be:

> From luxury symbol to cultural self-expression platform.

AI should help produce offline/online moments, not just looks.

### Best trust-and-retention fit: Kiehl's

Kiehl's is strong if the team prefers a more steady skincare relationship strategy:

> From apothecary skincare brand to personalized skin knowledge companion.

This route may be more feasible and retention-driven, but less visually explosive.

### Most original but complex: L'Oreal Professionnel

L'Oreal Professionnel could stand out because few teams may choose a professional hair brand. The strategic angle is strong:

> From salon service to AI-augmented stylist-consumer relationship.

But the B2B2C complexity and salon data/channel requirements increase execution risk.

## Suggested Next Step

Before final brand choice, the team should validate the top 3-4 brands on Chinese consumer platforms:

- Xiaohongshu: content volume, user language, pain points, look/style vocabulary
- Douyin: campaign and creator interaction patterns
- Tmall/JD: hero products, price bands, reviews, repurchase clues
- WeChat/private domain: membership or service model where visible

Recommended shortlist for deeper research:

1. 3CE
2. YSL Beauty
3. Kiehl's
4. L'Oreal Professionnel
